Parent Function\[ y = \frac{1}{x} \]
Transformed Model\[ f(x) = \frac{0.119}{-(x - 0.03)} + 1 \]

Transformations Applied

The standard y = 1/x curve was very steep for the relatively shallow curve of the plastic molded chair. To align the function's branches precisely with the physical plastic contours, transformations were applied to the parent function in a strict logical sequence based on the mapping structure \(a \cdot f(k(x-d)) + c\):

  • Horizontal Reflection (\(k = -1\)): First, a horizontal reflection was applied by introducing a negative sign directly to the horizontal tracking argument in the denominator. This transformation flips the reciprocal branches across the vertical axis, reversing their direction to perfectly match the real slope orientation of the chair backrest panels. The horizontal compression/stretch factor remains \(\frac{1}{|-1|} = 1\).
  • Vertical Compression (\(a = 0.119\)): Next, a vertical compression factor of 0.119 was introduced to scale the curve. This step flattens the naturally steep, aggressive trajectory of the standard hyperbola branches so that the mathematical path can smoothly hug the shallow edge of the molded plastic frame.
  • Horizontal Translation (\(d = 0.03\)): The function was then translated 0.03 units to the right, changing the denominator variable group to \(-(x - 0.03)\). This shift moves the vertical asymptote away from the origin, positioning the curve's bend to line up precisely with the physical peak of the backrest paneling.
  • Vertical Translation (\(c = 1\)): Finally, a vertical translation of 1 unit upward was applied to shift the entire model. This fields a baseline shift that lifts the function's horizontal asymptote away from the x-axis to align perfectly with the vertical displacement from the mid-quadricep coordinate layout.

Domain, Range, and Asymptotic Behaviour

The parent function \( y = \frac{1}{x} \) has a natural domain of \( D: \{x \in \mathbb{R} \mid x \neq 0\} \) and a range of \( R: \{y \in \mathbb{R} \mid y \neq 0\} \), meaning it splits infinitely around its central axes. Following our transformations, the vertical discontinuity shifts to \( x = 0.03 \) and the horizontal baseline asymptote settles at \( y = 1.0 \). This structural asymptote behavior means that as \(x \to 0.03^{-}\), \(f(x) \to \infty\), and as \(x \to 0.03^{+}\), \(f(x) \to -\infty\).

Vertical Asymptote\[ x = 0.03 \]
Horizontal Asymptote\[ y = 1.0 \]

The domain is strictly restricted to isolate only the specific curved parts of the backrest that can be accurately represented by the reciprocal function while avoiding the vertical asymptote at \(x=0.03\):

\[ D: \{x \in \mathbb{R} \mid -0.7 \le x \le -0.11875 \cup 0.268 \le x \le 0.9\} \]

The original unrestricted reciprocal model would continue infinitely near the asymptote, but the actual chair curve does not form an infinite spike. Therefore, the traced branch is clipped before it reaches the discontinuity. This keeps the two visible parts of the chair frame while excluding the undefined value at \(x=0.03\) and removing empty space that does not belong to the plastic molding.

  • Right-Branch Minimum Height: At \(x=0.268\), the model gives \(f(0.268)\approx0.5\), creating the lowest visible part of the traced chair curve.
  • Right-Branch Upper Boundary: At \(x=0.9\), the model gives \(f(0.9)\approx0.863\), where the right trace naturally ends on the frame.
  • Left-Branch Lower Boundary: At \(x=-0.7\), the model gives \(f(-0.7)\approx1.163\), which begins the upper-left visible curve.
  • Left-Branch Maximum Height: At \(x=-0.11875\), the model gives \(f(-0.11875)\approx1.8\), forming the highest visible boundary before the graph would rise too sharply toward the asymptote.

This strict boundary control successfully alters the infinite properties of the parent hyperbola into two realistic real-world intervals. It terminates the mathematical curves precisely where the plastic molding ends in the photograph while preserving the asymptotic behaviour that explains the sharp separation between the two visible branches.
