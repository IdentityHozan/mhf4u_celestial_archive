Transformations Applied

To accurately track the swift curve of the flying cape drawing on the bus mural, transformations were applied to the parent function \( y = \frac{1}{x^2} \) in strict mathematical order. To analyze horizontal alterations in standard mapping form, the denominator is evaluated based on its shift values:

  • Horizontal Translation (\( d = \sqrt{142} \approx 11.92 \)): The function was translated approximately 11.92 units to the right, changing the denominator structure to \( x^2 - 142 \). This shifts the parent function's natural vertical asymptote away from the origin to \( x = \sqrt{142} \). This positioning aligns the sharp drop of the right branch with the steep, outer edge of the character's flying cape.
  • Horizontal Compression/Stretch (\(k = 1\)): The horizontal scaling factor remains unchanged at a factor of \(\frac{1}{1} = 1\), preserving the core curve width of the parent function's single-branch path.
  • Vertical Translation (\( c = 3.3 \)): Next, the entire graph was shifted 3.3 units upward. This vertical translation elevates the curve's baseline to align with the physical height of the cape graphic relative to the lower edge of the bus chassis.

Physical Constraints and Asymptotic Behaviour

The parent function \( y = \frac{1}{x^2} \) has an infinite domain where \( x \neq 0 \), a range of \( y > 0 \), and a vertical asymptote running along the y-axis. In this transformed real-world model, the function contains two mathematical discontinuities where the denominator equals zero, producing vertical asymptotes at \( x = \pm\sqrt{142} \) (or roughly \( x \approx \pm 11.92 \)). Because our model utilizes only the positive branch, the key operational boundary is the vertical asymptote at \( x \approx 11.92 \). As values of \(x\) approach this threshold from the right, the outputs head toward positive infinity, perfectly capturing the nearly vertical upper edge of the cape graphic.

Vertical Asymptotes\[ x = \pm\sqrt{142} \approx \pm11.92 \]
Horizontal Asymptote\[ y = 3.3 \]

For the webpage presentation, the domain is strictly limited to the specific boundaries where the character's cape is actively drawn across the background panels:

\[ D: \{x \in \mathbb{R} \mid 11.94 \le x \le 14.2\} \]

Since the rational function is completely continuous and decreasing over this particular window, the maximum and minimum elevations are found directly by evaluating our tracking endpoints. By clipping the domain slightly to the right of the vertical asymptote at \(x = 11.94\), we prevent the function from extending into an unrealistic, infinite vertical spike.

  • Maximum Cape Height: The highest boundary point occurs near the asymptote at \( x = 11.94 \), where \( f(11.94) \approx 5.0743 \).
  • Minimum Cape Height: As the cape curve flattens out towards the right, evaluating the endpoint at \( x = 14.2 \) yields an approximate height of \( f(14.2) \approx 3.3168 \).

This contextual restriction alters the infinite range of the parent function into a restricted real-world interval. This prevents the mathematical model from extending up into empty air space or down past the physical layout coordinates where the illustration terminates.

Why This Model Fits and Rejected Alternatives

A reciprocal squared function was chosen because the cape curve rises steeply near one boundary and then gradually levels out as it moves away from the asymptote. This behaviour closely matches the right branch of a transformed \(y=\frac{1}{x^2}\) function. The denominator adjustment \(x^2-142\) creates vertical asymptotes at \(x=\pm\sqrt{142}\), and the model only uses the positive branch because that is the part that matches the visible cape. A linear model was rejected because the cape is clearly curved rather than straight. A quadratic model was also less suitable because it would form a rounded turning point instead of the steep asymptotic edge shown in the image. Therefore, the reciprocal squared model gives a more realistic mathematical explanation of the cape’s sharp drop and gradual flattening.
