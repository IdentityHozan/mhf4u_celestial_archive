Transformations Applied

The parent quartic function \( y = x^4 \) normally opens upward from the origin and slopes away symmetrically. To match the non-symmetrical rolling terrain of the mountain ridge, transformations were applied to the parent function in a strategic, iterative sequence using the factored root mapping parameters:

  • Vertical Reflection (\(a < 0\)): First, a vertical reflection across the horizontal axis was applied by introducing a negative sign to the leading coefficient (\(a = -\frac{3}{5}\)). This flips the infinite arms of the quartic function downward to match the descending landscape profiles.
  • Vertical Compression (\(|a| = \frac{3}{5}\)): A vertical compression by a factor of \(\frac{3}{5}\) was applied via the scale factor. This steps down the vertical growth rate, flattening the naturally aggressive trajectory of the polynomial curve so that it can smoothly track the gradual, rolling crest of the mountains without overshooting the actual horizon.
  • Horizontal Translations & Linear Factor Mapping (Root Positioning): Instead of a simple horizontal translation parameter \(d\), the independent variable was structurally adjusted to position real linear roots across separate intervals to capture the complex landscape contour. This involved shifting factors horizontally to place distinct zero crossings at \( x = -1.41 \) and \( x = 0.52 \). Crucially, a double root tangency point was created at \( x = -0.41 \) via the squared factor group \( (x + 0.41)^2 \), which mathematically forces the curve to smoothly turn around and bounce off its baseline exactly 0.41 units to the left of the central reference pillar, precisely recreating the localized valley dip.
  • Vertical Translation (\(c = 0.528\)): Finally, a vertical translation of 0.528 units upward was executed, adding 0.528 directly to the function. This lifts the entire model away from the horizontal axis to align the function's baseline height with the actual physical elevation of the mountain ridge high above the viewing platform handrail.

Physical Constraints and Real-World Restrictions

While the parent quartic function extends its domain infinitely across the coordinate plane, the physical mountain range is bounded by the architectural layout of the terrace. The domain of this model is strictly restricted to isolate the primary segment of the mountain ridge clearly visible on the left side of the frame: \[ D: \{x \in \mathbb{R} \mid -0.96 \le x \le 0.19\} \] This restriction suppresses the natural tendency of the polynomial branches to continue indefinitely outside the photograph. The parent function \(y=x^4\) normally has a domain of all real numbers and a range of \(y\ge0\). However, this transformed quartic is reflected, vertically compressed, translated upward, and restricted to the visible mountain ridge only.

Over the selected interval, the curve reaches its lowest contextual value at the double-root turning point near \(x=-0.41\), where the squared factor \((x+0.41)^2\) becomes zero. This produces \(f(-0.41)=0.528\). The highest visible boundary occurs near the left endpoint, where \(f(-0.96) \approx 0.6489\). The right endpoint remains slightly lower, with \(f(0.19) \approx 0.6420\), so it does not determine the maximum of the restricted range.

  • Minimum Ridge Height: At the double-root valley point \(x=-0.41\), \(f(-0.41)=0.528\). This represents the lowest visible dip of the mountain contour within the selected window.
  • Maximum Ridge Height: At the left boundary \(x=-0.96\), \(f(-0.96) \approx 0.6489\). This represents the highest tracked part of the visible ridge segment before the model is cut off by the photograph's frame and handrail reference area.

Therefore, the infinite behaviour of the quartic parent function is converted into a bounded real-world model. The domain restriction prevents the polynomial from extending into areas where no mountain ridge is being traced, while the range captures only the actual vertical displacement of the visible mountain contour above the handrail baseline.
